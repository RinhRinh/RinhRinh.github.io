The Challenge: Sea Level Rise in South Florida

South Florida is on the front lines of climate change, with sea level rise posing an immediate and significant threat to our coastal communities. The region is uniquely vulnerable due to its low elevation, porous limestone bedrock, and dense coastal development.

Target Audience:

This resource is designed for South Florida residents, local business owners, community leaders, and policymakers who need practical information about how sea level rise is affecting our region and what can be done to adapt.

The Science Behind Sea Level Rise

Global warming is causing sea levels to rise through two main mechanisms: thermal expansion (as ocean water warms, it expands) and the melting of land-based ice sheets and glaciers.

In South Florida, these global trends are compounded by local factors, including land subsidence (sinking) and the region's porous limestone foundation.

Historical and Projected Rise

Sea levels in South Florida have risen about 8 inches since 1950. According to NOAA and the Southeast Florida Regional Climate Change Compact, we can expect:

  • 10-17 inches of additional rise by 2040
  • 21-54 inches by 2070
  • 40-136 inches by 2120

Impact on Our Communities

Sea level rise isn't just a future concernâ€”it's affecting South Florida communities today through various mechanisms that will intensify over time.

Sunny Day Flooding

Even without rain, high tides now regularly cause flooding in low-lying areas of Miami Beach, Fort Lauderdale, and the Florida Keys.

These "king tides" damage infrastructure, disrupt transportation, and impact businesses as saltwater inundates streets and sidewalks.

Saltwater Intrusion

Rising sea levels are pushing saltwater into South Florida's freshwater Biscayne Aquifer, threatening drinking water supplies for millions of residents.

This issue is exacerbated by the region's porous limestone bedrock, which allows seawater to penetrate inland from below.

Property Values & Insurance

Properties in vulnerable areas are facing declining values, difficulty obtaining affordable insurance, and challenges securing mortgages.

This "climate gentrification" is forcing many residents to relocate, altering neighborhood demographics and local economies.

Beach Erosion & Tourism

Florida's beaches are eroding at an accelerating rate, threatening the $100+ billion tourism industry that depends on pristine coastal environments.

Higher sea levels allow storm surges to reach further inland, causing greater damage during hurricane season.

Compounding Effects

Sea level rise amplifies the impact of extreme weather events. Even a modest hurricane can cause significantly more flooding when starting from a higher baseline sea level.

Vulnerable Communities

While sea level rise affects all South Floridians, certain communities bear disproportionate burdens due to geographic, economic, and social factors.

Low-Income Neighborhoods

Many lower-income communities lack the resources for flood prevention infrastructure or property elevation.

These neighborhoods often have fewer green spaces to absorb floodwater and older drainage systems that cannot handle increased water volumes.

Residents may not have the financial means to relocate or rebuild after flooding events.

Elderly Population

South Florida has a large elderly population, many living in coastal communities and on fixed incomes.

Seniors may face challenges evacuating during emergencies and are more vulnerable to health impacts from flooding events.

Rising insurance costs and property taxes can force elderly residents from homes they've lived in for decades.

Small Businesses

Local businesses in flood-prone areas face increased operating costs, property damage, and interrupted operations.

Many small businesses lack flood insurance or the capital needed to implement resilience measures.

Repeated flooding can drive away customers and force businesses to close or relocate.

Climate Gentrification

As coastal properties face increasing flood risks, property values in higher-elevation areas are rising rapidly. This "climate gentrification" is displacing long-time residents of historically affordable neighborhoods like Little Haiti and Liberty City in Miami, which sit on higher ground.

Engineering Solutions & Adaptation Strategies

While we can't stop sea level rise immediately, South Florida communities can implement a range of solutions to adapt to changing conditions and protect people, property, and ecosystems.

Infrastructure Improvements

  • Installing pumping systems to remove floodwater from streets and neighborhoods
  • Building seawalls and surge barriers to protect critical areas
  • Elevating roads and critical infrastructure above projected flood levels
  • Upgrading stormwater management systems with backflow preventers and increased capacity

Nature-Based Solutions

  • Restoring mangroves and wetlands that act as natural buffers against storm surge
  • Creating living shorelines that combine vegetation with structural elements
  • Building artificial reefs to break wave energy before it reaches shore
  • Expanding green spaces in urban areas to absorb stormwater

Building Adaptations

  • Elevating existing structures above base flood elevation
  • Building new construction on pilings to allow water to flow underneath
  • Installing flood vents to reduce pressure on foundations during floods
  • Using flood-resistant building materials on lower floors
  • Creating deployable flood barriers for doors and windows

Policy & Planning

  • Updating building codes to account for future sea level projections
  • Creating special assessment districts to fund adaptation projects
  • Developing managed retreat strategies for highly vulnerable areas
  • Establishing revolving loan funds to help property owners implement resilience measures
  • Integrating sea level rise projections into all infrastructure planning

Success Story: Miami Beach's Pump Program

Miami Beach has installed over 80 stormwater pumps and raised numerous roads, reducing flooding in previously vulnerable neighborhoods by up to 80%. While expensive ($500+ million), these investments are protecting billions in property value and preserving quality of life for residents.

Adaptation Timeline: A Phased Approach

Short-Term (Now-2030)

  • Install backflow preventers on stormwater systems
  • Raise critical infrastructure and roadways
  • Implement flood monitoring systems
  • Begin mangrove restoration projects

Medium-Term (2030-2050)

  • Comprehensive seawall and surge barrier systems
  • Large-scale stormwater improvements
  • Transition to elevated construction in vulnerable areas
  • Expand living shoreline initiatives

Long-Term (2050-2100)

  • Implement managed retreat from most vulnerable areas
  • Create floating infrastructure where appropriate
  • Comprehensive redesign of coastal urban areas
  • Regional freshwater management solutions

Get Involved: Taking Action

Addressing sea level rise requires action at all levelsâ€”from individual homeowners to regional governments. Here's how you can be part of the solution:

For Homeowners

  • Assess your property's flood risk using FEMA maps and sea level projection tools
  • Consider flood-proofing measures like elevating utilities and installing check valves

  • Implement resilience measures for facilities and equipment
